After 200+ inspections across Gurgaon, we've started to notice patterns. Certain sectors — and certain building types within those sectors — come up disproportionately in our high-priority seepage findings. Here's what the data shows and, more importantly, why.

---

## The sectors with the highest seepage rates in our inspections

**Sectors 4–17 (Old Gurgaon):** These are older residential areas with buildings constructed in the 1980s–2000s. The waterproofing materials used in that era — bitumen-based coatings, standard cement plaster — have a service life of 15–20 years. Most of it has now exceeded that. In our inspections of buildings over 20 years old in these sectors, seepage comes up in over 70% of flats.

**DLF Phases 1 and 2 (Sectors 24–28):** Paradoxically, some of the most expensive residential areas in Gurgaon have high seepage rates. The reason: many of these buildings were constructed quickly during the Gurgaon construction boom of the late 1990s and early 2000s, with waterproofing quality that didn't match the price point. The buildings are also now 20–25 years old.

**Sectors 46–49 (Rapid construction era):** This belt saw enormous construction activity in 2008–2014. Buildings from this era are now hitting the age where terrace waterproofing and external wall sealant failures are starting to show. We flag seepage in roughly 40% of flats in buildings from this period.

---

## What's causing it

The seepage we find in Gurgaon properties almost always comes from one of three sources:

**Terrace / roof failure.** The most common. Terrace waterproofing is a maintenance item that most RWAs either delay or do poorly. A poor terrace repair — surface-applied patch rather than a proper membrane — will hold for one or two monsoons and then fail. Once the membrane fails, water finds its way into the slab and then into the flat below, often emerging at the ceiling of the bathroom or at the top of walls.

**External wall degradation.** Exposed external walls — particularly northwest-facing walls, which take the most driving rain in Delhi's monsoon — develop microcracks in the plaster over time. Water infiltrates the cracks, saturates the wall, and appears on internal surfaces. This is what the moisture meter catches even when it's been painted over.

**Plumbing leaks in common areas.** In multi-storey buildings, a slow leak in the water supply pipes or drainage stacks of the flat above yours can appear as seepage in your flat — in the bathroom ceiling, at the junction of walls, or under kitchen counters. This is less common but worth checking when the source isn't obvious.

---

## What to watch for when viewing a flat

**Freshly painted walls on the northwest side.** In isolation, fresh paint is fine. Combined with a view from a building with visible external water staining, or in a flat that was recently vacated, it warrants a moisture meter check.

**Bathroom ceiling condition in top-floor flats.** Discolouration, any sign of previous patching, or a ceiling that has been recently repainted when the rest of the flat hasn't — these all suggest a history of terrace-related seepage.

**The RWA maintenance record.** Ask specifically about terrace waterproofing. When was the last terrace waterproofing done? By whom? What method? A good RWA will know. An evasive answer tells you what you need to know.

---

## The practical implication

If you're viewing a flat in Old Gurgaon or in a DLF Phase 1–2 building, or in any building constructed between 2005 and 2014, a moisture meter check is not optional — it's essential. These areas have the highest rates of concealed seepage in our inspection data.

The good news: seepage, once found, is almost always negotiable. Most landlords would rather make a commitment to fix it than lose a tenant over it. The problem is finding it before you've already signed.
